Session 1 of 6

What You Were Actually Promised

~12 minutes teaching + 10-minute written exercise

← Previous Session Session 2 →

Someone made you a promise when you started meditating. It might have been a formal promise — a clinical study referenced in a book, a teacher's explanation of what consistent practice produces. More likely it was informal: the general impression you picked up from a combination of sources. Practice every day. Notice your thoughts. Return to the breath. And over time, the chronic stress lifts. You get better at dealing with difficult emotions. You become less reactive. You're more present for the people you care about.

Most of that is true. I want to say that clearly, because this course is not a debunking. The benefits of meditation are real, replicable, and documented across a serious body of research. People who practice consistently do report reductions in perceived stress. They describe changes in how they relate to difficult emotions. The neuroimaging literature shows structural and functional changes in experienced practitioners. None of that is marketing. It's real.

But the promise is usually handed to you as a package — benefits, no downside, more is better, everyone is a candidate. And that is where honest accounting tends to go missing.

Here is what the research is more careful about than the popular framing: the benefits are real, but they are not uniform. They depend on what problem you're starting from. They have a dose-response relationship — which means there are doses above which the evidence for additional benefit gets thin. There are documented adverse effects — not rare or exotic, but recurring patterns in committed practitioners: emotional flattening, loss of motivation, in some cases a kind of depersonalization that doesn't feel like insight. And there is almost no serious conversation in mainstream mindfulness circles about when practice might not be indicated, or when you might have had enough.

I'm not interested in tearing that down. I'm interested in what happens when we relate to the practice honestly — with clear eyes about what it does, what it doesn't do, and what it might cost at higher doses or over longer timescales.

Here is why that matters to me: the alternative is motivated contact with the practice. You practice because you have a belief about what it should be doing to you, and every difficult experience either confirms that belief or gets labeled as evidence that you're not practicing correctly. A lot of people spend years in that loop. You can see it in the questions practitioners ask teachers: "Why am I still anxious after five years of daily practice?" The question assumes the promise was real and the failure is personal. Honest accounting starts earlier — with what you were actually promised, and whether that promise held.

The second problem with motivated contact is that it makes the practice fragile. If the practice needs to be working the way you expect it to work in order to justify continuing, then any honest observation that complicates the picture becomes a threat. That's a bad foundation for something you're going to spend years with. What I'd rather offer — what this course is trying to offer — is a different relationship to the practice entirely: one that can survive honest inspection because it's built on what's actually happening rather than what should be happening.

That's harder, at first. It asks you to set aside a story that might be serving you well enough that questioning it feels unnecessary. But I think it's the only version of practice worth having in the long run. And in my experience, once you start looking clearly at what your practice is actually doing — as opposed to what it's supposed to be doing — the picture is more interesting, not less.

Practice: The Expectation Inventory

This session's practice is a written exercise. Set aside ten minutes with a pen and something to write on.

Three questions:

First: Write down three things you expected practice to change when you started. Be specific. Not "I expected to feel better" but something you can actually track: "I expected to stop ruminating about the same anxieties every morning" or "I expected to feel less reactive in difficult conversations." Three specific things.

Second: For each of those three, write down honestly whether it changed. Not whether you got something out of practice in general — but whether that specific thing changed, in the way you expected.

Third: Write down three things that actually changed that you didn't expect. These matter more than they might seem. They're the real effects — the ones the promise didn't predict.

Take the full ten minutes. Write it down before you continue.

[Pause — allow 10 minutes for the written inventory.]

What you just wrote is the starting point for everything else in this course. Not as a verdict on whether your practice has been worthwhile — but as a baseline. An honest look at what has actually happened, as distinct from what was supposed to happen.

The rest of this course is going to keep asking you to do that: to look directly at your practice rather than at the story about your practice. It's a small shift in orientation. But it's the difference between using meditation as a belief system and using it as a tool you understand.

That's what the research actually offers. Not a guarantee. Not a universal cure. A well-documented set of effects, for particular kinds of problems, at particular doses, in particular people. We'll spend the next five sessions understanding what those effects are — and what to do with that understanding.

Session Summary

The session examines the gap between how mindfulness is marketed — uniformly beneficial, more is better, everyone is a candidate — and what you have actually experienced. The key insight is that the research treats practice more like a drug than a vitamin: real effects at real doses, but not universal, not unlimited, and not without documented costs. The written exercise asks you to name three specific things you expected practice to change, assess honestly whether each one changed, and identify three things that actually changed that you didn't expect. Carry this inventory into the next session as your baseline — a record of what has actually happened, as distinct from what was supposed to happen.
