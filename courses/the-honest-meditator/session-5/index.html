Session 5 of 6

Later-Stage Failure Modes

~15 minutes teaching + 10-minute written exercise

← Session 4 Session 6 →

The 60-second hold from last session is a moment-level check. Before you release a difficult emotional state, you pause long enough to ask whether it's carrying information you'd want to keep. Most of the time, the answer comes quickly — it's noise, release it, the information lives elsewhere — and you move on.

What I want to focus on today is what happens when you run that check consistently over months, and you start seeing something at a higher level. Not just "is this particular state carrying information?" but: is my practice, taken as a whole, systematically directing me away from something? Is there a pattern here, and not just a moment?

That is a different question, and it requires a different kind of attention.

There are two broad categories of failure in contemplative practice, and they tend to occur at different stages.

Early-stage failure modes are well-mapped. Distraction, inconsistency, magical thinking about dramatic experiences, using the practice as mood regulation without understanding it as a practice at all. These are what teachers talk about most, and they're genuinely common, but they're also relatively transparent — you can usually feel them for what they are, and they respond to more practice, more instruction, more time.

Late-stage failure modes are different in kind. They occur specifically because practice has become more advanced, not less. They don't feel like failures from inside. They can feel, with some accuracy, like attainment. And that phenomenological overlap is what makes them worth naming directly, because without naming them, they can operate without interference for years.

I want to describe three of them in enough detail that you can recognize them if they're active.

The first is spiritual bypassing. The term has been around long enough to be somewhat familiar, but it's worth saying what it actually is, rather than what the caricature suggests.

It isn't using practice as an excuse to feel superior. That happens, but it's not what I mean here. The more common version is subtler: using the moves of practice to avoid engaging something difficult that genuinely requires engagement. You have a hard conversation you've been avoiding. There is a decision that keeps getting deferred. There is a piece of work you're not doing. And when the discomfort of those things arises — as it does, because they're real — you meet it with awareness. You watch it arise and pass. You don't chase the story. You return to the present moment.

This is technically the practice, done correctly. From the outside, from the inside, it looks and feels like meditation. The problem is what it isn't doing: engaging the situation. The conversation is still unhad. The decision is still deferred. The work is still not done. And each time you successfully metabolize the discomfort of that, the gap between you and the thing that needs your attention quietly widens.

The tell is in the situation, not the state. If, after months of skillfully meeting the discomfort around something, that something is exactly where you left it — not because you've genuinely decided it doesn't require action, but because the practice keeps making the immediate discomfort manageable — then the practice may be functioning as avoidance, not engagement.

The second failure mode is motivational erosion.

This is what session two was about in theory. Now it shows up as a lived pattern.

You have worked, over some extended period, at relating to difficult emotional states with more equanimity. And you have mostly succeeded. The anxiety about a long-term project is more metabolizable. The discomfort about a drifting career is less acute. The loneliness that used to bite more sharply is now something you can meet without immediately reorganizing your life around it.

This is not the practice failing. This is the practice working, more or less as advertised. But notice what has also happened: the thing hasn't changed. The project is still in the same place. The career is still drifting. The loneliness is still structural. Only your relationship to those facts has changed. And after a while, you may notice that you are not especially organized around changing them.

The question this raises is not whether equanimity is good — it is, often, better than the alternative. The question is whether the equanimity has become decoupled from the situation that warranted it. Equanimity about a problem you have actually addressed is peace. Equanimity about a problem you haven't addressed is something else. It can look and feel quite similar.

What distinguishes them, from the inside, is almost nothing. That's the problem.

The third failure mode is equanimity as indifference.

This one is the most difficult to describe precisely, because it sounds, on the surface, like a contradiction. Equanimity is supposed to be a good thing — and in many contexts it is. What I am pointing at is a specific way it can shade into something that looks like equanimity but functions differently.

Intimacy requires friction. Real commitment requires feeling the stakes. Meaningful, sustained work requires caring — specifically, caring enough that the result actually matters to you. These are not mystical claims. They are observations about how motivation works in practice.

A practitioner who has genuinely developed the capacity to remain spacious around difficult outcomes has something valuable. They are also, potentially, someone who can stay out of situations that require them to fully commit — where the discomfort of not knowing, of caring and possibly failing, of being actually vulnerable, would previously have organized their behavior. The spaciousness and the indifference have different causes. But in some situations they produce the same result: you don't fully go in.

This does not mean the answer is to become less spacious. It means it's worth asking, in the specific places where it matters, whether the equanimity is a response to a situation you've genuinely assessed and decided to accept — or whether it's functioning as a way to stay comfortable in a situation that would otherwise require something uncomfortable of you.

The diagnostic difficulty with all three of these is that they share a surface texture with their opposite. Spiritual bypassing feels like practice. Motivational erosion feels like peace. Equanimity as indifference feels like spaciousness. From inside these states, you are not necessarily doing anything that feels wrong. You may, in fact, be doing something that feels notably right — calm, centered, clear, present.

This is why the audit this session asks for is a behavioral one, not a phenomenological one. You cannot reliably distinguish genuine equanimity from practiced avoidance by how it feels. You can distinguish them by looking at what you're actually doing, or not doing, and whether the pattern is one you would endorse on examination.

That examination is the practice component.

Practice: Honest Audit

This session's practice is a written exercise. Set aside ten minutes. Write in full sentences — not bullet points, not shorthand. The precision of the writing is part of the practice.

First: Is there anything in your life you have stopped feeling pressure about that you probably should still feel pressure about? Not noise — real pressure. A relationship that needs attention. A career decision that keeps drifting. A conversation that would require something uncomfortable of you. A project you care about that isn't moving. Name it specifically. Don't let it stay abstract.

Second: Is there a difficult conversation, decision, or piece of work you have been accepting rather than engaging? Write the specific thing, not the category. Not "relationships" — which relationship, which conversation, how long has it been sitting. Not "work projects" — which one, what would engaging it actually require?

Third: For the thing you named in question two — how would you tell the difference between genuine equanimity and practiced avoidance? What evidence would distinguish them? Write the answer before you decide which one it is.

[Pause — allow 10 minutes for the written exercise.]

The point of this audit is not to produce guilt or to invalidate what you have built in practice. Most of what you've built is real and useful. The point is to run a check that the practice itself doesn't automatically run — a behavioral accounting of whether the equanimity is tracking something you've genuinely assessed, or covering something you haven't.

The next session takes everything we've covered and asks a different kind of question: not what's wrong with the practice, but what an honest practice actually looks like — calibrated, intentional, with clear eyes about what you're doing and why.

Session Summary

The session examines three ways a well-developed practice can start to function as something other than what it was built for: spiritual bypassing (using the moves of practice to avoid engaging what requires engagement), motivational erosion (equanimity about a problem you haven't addressed), and equanimity as indifference (spaciousness that functions as a way to stay comfortable in situations that require genuine commitment). The key insight is that all three share a surface texture with their opposite — they feel like attainment, not failure — so the diagnostic is behavioral, not phenomenological. The written exercise asks you to name, in specific terms, what you've stopped feeling pressure about that still warrants pressure, and what you may have been accepting rather than engaging. Carry this audit forward: what does an honest practice actually look like from here?
