The Honest Meditator

Student FAQ

Who is this course for? And who is it not for?

It's for people who already meditate — not beginners, not people considering starting. Specifically, it's for practitioners who have spent enough time on the cushion to have noticed things the standard material doesn't quite account for. The anxiety that practice was supposed to dissolve and hasn't. The question underneath the practice that doesn't have an obvious home. The gap between what you were told to expect and what's actually happened.

It is not for beginners. If you don't have an existing practice, this course will not make sense — it assumes familiarity with meditation as a baseline, not a destination. It is also not for people who want technique refinement, guided practice, or general encouragement. If you're looking for those, there is excellent material available elsewhere. This course picks up where that material stops.

Do I need an existing meditation practice? How much experience?

Yes, an existing practice is required. The course doesn't teach you how to meditate. It examines what meditation is doing to you, assumes you've been doing it long enough to have experienced the nuances, and builds from there.

How much experience? There's no minimum number of hours or years. The real threshold is whether you've sat with the practice long enough to notice something the standard framing doesn't explain — a question that hasn't gone away, a gap between the promise and your actual experience. Most people who find this course useful have been practicing regularly for at least a year, but that's a rough indicator, not a requirement. If you're brand new and curious, come back after you've built some practice. The questions this course asks won't land without the experience to ground them.

Is this a guided meditation course?

No. This is a teaching and examination course. There are no guided meditations, no breathwork sequences, no technique refinements. Each of the six sessions is approximately 12–15 minutes of teaching, followed by a structured written exercise. The teaching covers what the practice is actually doing — the acceptance paradox, dosage, failure modes — and the exercises ask you to examine those questions in your own practice, in writing.

If you're looking for a guided meditation course, this is not it. The assumption here is that you already know how to meditate and that what would be most useful is honest accounting of what you're doing, not more instruction on how to do it.

How long does each session take?

Plan for about 25 minutes per session. Each session includes roughly 12–15 minutes of teaching and a 10-minute written exercise. Total teaching time across all six sessions is approximately 84–90 minutes. Total course time, including the written exercises, is roughly 2.5 hours.

The written exercises are not optional. They are where the actual examination happens. Reading the teaching without doing the exercise is like reading a diagnostic without looking at your own data. You can do it, but you're leaving most of the course on the table.

What are the written exercises? Do I need to share them with anyone?

Each session ends with a structured written exercise — usually three to four prompts and ten minutes. The prompts are specific: not "reflect on your practice" but questions like "What specific problem were you trying to solve when you started meditating? Have you solved it?" or "Is there a difficult conversation you have been accepting instead of engaging?"

You don't share them with anyone. They're private. The course has no community component, no cohort, no accountability partner. The work is between you and the page.

The final exercise is a personal practice contract — a half-page document stating what your practice is currently solving, how you'd know if it stopped, and one thing you commit not to use mindfulness to avoid in the next month. You keep it. Revise it when it no longer fits.

Can I do sessions out of order?

Not productively. The course builds deliberately. Session 2's acceptance paradox presupposes the honest inventory from Session 1. Sessions 4 and 5 — distinguishing signal from noise, diagnosing failure modes — only make sense in the context of the dosage question Session 3 introduces. The personal practice contract in Session 6 is the culmination of everything that precedes it; without that prior work it's just a blank form.

If you listen to a session out of order and the references seem disconnected, that's why.

What do I do if the course surfaces something uncomfortable?

Stay with it long enough to read it. That's actually the core skill the course is teaching — the 60-second hold from Session 4, the honest audit from Session 5. Something uncomfortable coming up is not a sign the course is going wrong. It's often the opposite.

That said: if what comes up is clinical-level — acute depression, an anxiety disorder, trauma that has been dormant — this course is not the right tool. It doesn't offer therapeutic support and isn't designed to. A therapist is a better first resource for anything that has that quality. This course is for the philosophical and motivational friction of an established practice, not for mental health crises.

How is this different from other mindfulness courses?

Most mindfulness courses ask you to deepen your practice. This one asks you to examine it.

Where other courses offer reassurance and techniques, this one offers honest accounting: what the research actually shows (including the parts that get left out of popular framing), what deep acceptance practice does to motivation and felt stakes, when the instruction to "begin again" is right and when it might be skipping past something worth keeping. It addresses late-stage failure modes that committed practitioners encounter and that almost no one discusses, because the language for them hasn't fully developed yet.

The course trusts you to handle honest material. It doesn't soften the harder implications of the acceptance paradox or treat documented adverse effects as edge cases that won't apply to you. If you're looking for encouragement to keep practicing, this course will not provide it. If you're looking for clear-eyed accounting of what you're doing and why, it will.

Do I need any equipment or apps?

No. Just a way to read and something to write with — pen and paper is fine, digital is fine. No meditation cushion required, no app subscription, no special setup.

What happens after Session 6?

You leave with a personal practice contract you wrote for yourself. It's half a page: what problem your practice is currently solving, how you'd know if it stopped, what you're willing to question, and one specific thing you commit not to use mindfulness to avoid in the next month.

There's no follow-up material, no ongoing curriculum, no next course that picks up here. The contract is meant to be a reference point you return to on your own — revised when your situation changes, checked when you're not sure whether you're practicing or avoiding. The course ends; the honest practice continues.
