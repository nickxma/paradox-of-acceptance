The Cherry-Picking Problem

~6 min read

My father spent his career running on exactly the kind of motivation that mindfulness is supposed to dissolve. Fear of failure. Status anxiety. A chip on his shoulder the size of a career. Trying to explain non-dual meditation to him, I found myself making an argument I couldn't finish.

The insight is simple enough to describe: thoughts arise on their own. The self who seems to author them is itself just another appearance. The anxiety, the rumination, the loop of self-criticism — none of it belongs to a "you" that's actually running the show. Once you've seen this, the unnecessary suffering drops away.

My father listened. Then he asked the question I didn't have an answer for.

"What about the ambition?"

The Move

The unspoken assumption behind most mindfulness and performance discourse goes like this: you can dissolve the parts of the self that cause suffering while leaving the parts that generate energy intact. You can be free of insecurity without being free of drive. You can release the anxious self and keep the ambitious one.

This is the cherry-pick. It is nearly universal in practice communities, and almost never named.

The practitioners who describe this most honestly aren't lying. They've genuinely experienced something. They sit with difficult emotions more cleanly than they used to. The recursive self-criticism has quieted. Certain decisions come from a less frantic place. The practice has done real work.

What they tend not to notice — or not to say — is that the ambitious self hasn't been examined with the same rigor. The driven self, the one that still checks the follower count and still competes and still cares how the story turns out: that self has been quietly preserved. Not because it's been spared from the insight. Because it's been exempted from scrutiny.

The Atheist Analogy

Once you've genuinely seen that thoughts think themselves, the logic doesn't stop at the edges of your neurosis.

The structure here is identical to being an atheist about every god except one. You can't apply the critique of selective deity and then exempt your favorite. The argument either holds or it doesn't. The same is true with the non-dual insight. If the neurotic self is a construction — arising without a real author, maintained by habit, dissolved by direct seeing — then the motivated self is the same construction. They aren't two separate selves. They're the same process of identification, viewed from different angles.

The non-dual teaching doesn't say "there is no anxious self, but there is an ambitious one." It says there is no self. Period. Any reading that carves out a protected zone — that dissolves the inconvenient ego while preserving the useful one — has already departed from the teaching it claims to be following.

This isn't a minor variation. It's the whole argument in one move. Practitioners who want the liberation without the loss are asking the insight to do something the insight doesn't do.

The Teachers Who See It

A few teachers name this directly, though not always with full force.

Joko Beck, one of the most rigorous voices in American Zen, describes the core problem this way: the moment you fully surrender to direct experience — when you genuinely stop being a separate observer and become the sensation itself — you disappear. And she notes, with some dry honesty, that the entire project of a human life is oriented precisely toward not disappearing. Toward glorifying the self, not dissolving it.

She isn't saying this as a reason to stop practicing. She's naming the structural conflict. The practice asks for something that runs directly against what most people, most of the time, actually want from their lives.

James Low takes it further. Even the most selfless-sounding motivation — the bodhisattva vow, the commitment to liberate all beings — is still a sequence of intentions formed by a self projecting into a future. Still dualistic. If you're serious about the non-dual insight, he suggests, you can't exempt your noblest aspirations from the same scrutiny you apply to your pettiest ones. The compassion project gets the same treatment as the status project. There are no exceptions.

Why the Community Can't Say This

The most honest moment I know of from any prominent teacher on this problem goes roughly like this: yes, in principle, you could have the functional aspect of ego without the identity aspect. The operating principle without the sense that it belongs to a solid self. Then, almost immediately: well, good luck living your life that way.

Adyashanti knew the joke was on the theory. He described the possibility and then laughed.

The community can't say "you can't cherry-pick" because it would collapse the value proposition. People come to practice to feel better, not to feel less. They come to reduce the anxiety and keep the ambition. If you tell them the insight doesn't honor that distinction, most of them leave. The business of meditation instruction depends, at some level, on promising that the surgery is more targeted than it actually is.

Joan Tollifson, one of the more careful voices in the non-dual tradition, acknowledges the concession most teachers are quietly making: you need a working sense of self to function. Some sense of location, some psychological scaffolding, is simply required to operate. No matter how deep the practice goes, this doesn't go away.

But notice what this concession does to the teaching. If some sense of self inevitably remains — if the teaching knows this and builds around it — then the claim that there is "no self" is either describing something other than what it sounds like, or it's a teaching that doesn't fully mean what it says.

What It Actually Looks Like

The concrete picture is not flattering, but it is honest.

The meditator who can sit with a painful emotion for forty minutes still works eighty-hour weeks chasing a promotion. The practitioner who has "seen through the self" still checks their follower count. The teacher who speaks about non-attachment still runs a business.

These aren't failures of practice. They're evidence that cherry-picking is what everyone actually does, because the alternative — applying the insight without exception, equally to every domain — is functionally unlivable. The meditator would stop competing. The practitioner would stop tracking. The teacher would close the business. No one wants that. So instead, a partial truce. The ego gets dissolved in the domains where it causes the most visible pain. It gets preserved quietly in the domains where it generates the most visible energy. The whole arrangement gets called awakening.

The Honest Position

The non-dual insight either applies universally or it isn't the insight it claims to be. But applying it universally dissolves the agent who would apply it. This isn't a problem that practice can solve. It's a feature of the territory.

Most practitioners, if they're honest, are doing something much messier than the teachings describe. Negotiating a selective truce with their own psychology. Keeping what works. Releasing what hurts. Moving through the world as a partly-examined self who knows more than they did and less than they claim.

That's probably fine. It might even be the best available outcome.

But it's a different project than the one on the label. And the gap between what the teaching promises and what it actually asks for — that gap is not a misunderstanding to be clarified. It's the thing the community most consistently refuses to examine directly.

You can't be an atheist about every god except one.
